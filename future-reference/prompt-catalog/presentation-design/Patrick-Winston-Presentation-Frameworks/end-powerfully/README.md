# END ANY PRESENTATION POWERFULLY

## When to Use

Use this prompt to design your **final 60 seconds and closing slide**. Apply it in **Phase 4 (Polish)** of the presentation-mastery workflow, after your deck is nearly complete but before final submission.

This is your last chance to leave the audience with something memorable, not forgettable.

## Why It Works

Most presentations end with "Thank you" or "Questions?" — both wasted real estate. The final slide is the most memorable (primacy/recency effect). Patrick Winston's framework ensures you end on strength: contributions (what you're proposing), not gratitude (what you're not proposing).

The closing must also mirror the opening promise—audience hears the promise, you deliver proof, and they're left remembering the outcome.

## Trade-Offs

**What it does well:**
- Ensures final slide is contributions, not "questions"
- Creates closing words that salute audience instead of dismissing them
- Mirrors opening promise (circle closed, satisfaction)
- Gives you exact words for final 60 seconds (not improvised)
- Leaves lasting impression instead of forgettable ending

**What it sacrifices:**
- Requires courage to not end with "thank you" (feels polite but reads weak)
- Final slide must be specific (vague contributions don't work)
- Closing words must match your voice (can't be overly formal or casual)

## Related Techniques

- **Primacy/Recency:** Final slide is most memorable—make it count
- **Closed Loop:** Opening promise + closing contributions create satisfaction
- **Audience Salute:** Valuing audience over self-promotion
- **Call-to-Action:** Clear next step or decision required

## Example Use Case

**Scenario:** Your autonomous delivery pitch.

**Opening promise:** "By the end, you'll know which customer segments benefit most, the economics that work, and a pilot that proves it."

**Contributions slide:**
```
WHAT WE'RE PROPOSING

✓ 90-day pilot in [City] targeting [% of orders]
✓ Success metrics: 97% delivery rate, $2.50 unit margin, 4.5★ customer satisfaction
✓ Clear decision point: Scale or pause based on data

Budget: $[X] | Timeline: [Days] | ROI: $[Y] by Q4
```

**Closing words:** "You have the data, you have the thesis, you know what we'd learn in 90 days. The question isn't whether autonomous delivery works—it's whether we're brave enough to test it on our terms, in our way, with our customers. I'm asking for that permission."

**Final 60 seconds:** Contributions slide on screen, you deliver closing words standing to the side (not reading), audience sees the slide + hears your conviction.

## How to Use This Prompt

1. Clarify: What's the ONE thing you want them to remember?
2. Run prompt: Input topic + key takeaway
3. Draft: Contributions slide (specific, concrete, actionable)
4. Draft: Closing words (audience salute + call-to-action)
5. Practice: Final 60 seconds with slide + speaking

## Common Pitfalls

- **Weak contributions slide:** "Thank you for listening" → Meaningless. Be specific: approval, budget, decision.
- **Closing words too formal:** "I appreciate your time" → Distant. Use: "I'm asking for..." or "Here's what we'll learn..."
- **No connection to opening:** Opening promised X, closing asks for Y → Audience confused
- **Improvising final words:** "I'll think of something" → Risks stumbling. Script it, practice it.
- **Ending with questions:** "Any questions?" → Weak. Contributions slide stays up, questions happen, but you're not defined by Q&A.
