# USE PROPS AND STORIES TO TEACH ANYTHING

## When to Use

Use this prompt to teach **complex or abstract concepts** in a memorable way. Apply it in **Phase 3 (Content)** of the presentation-mastery workflow when you have slides that explain difficult ideas and want to make them tangible.

This prompt is especially powerful for technical concepts, novel approaches, or anything your audience hasn't encountered before.

## Why It Works

Humans learn through multiple channels: words, visuals, and physical sensation. A prop activates all three simultaneously. A prop + story creates an unforgettable teaching moment.

Patrick Winston's framework recognizes that the best demonstrations:
1. Create genuine confusion or tension first (so resolution is satisfying)
2. Use something physical you can point to/manipulate
3. Guide attention explicitly ("Watch this part")
4. Allow failure (failure teaches as much as success)

## Trade-Offs

**What it does well:**
- Makes abstract ideas concrete and physical
- Creates memorable teaching moments (people remember props, not bullet points)
- Engages multiple learning channels at once
- Allows you to handle objections ("But what if X?")
- Works even if it fails (failure is informative, not embarrassing)

**What it sacrifices:**
- Requires preparation and practice (not spontaneous)
- Props may not exist for all ideas (need to get creative)
- Takes stage time (only use for truly complex ideas, not everything)
- Requires confidence to pull off (awkwardness kills the effect)

## Related Techniques

- **Experiential Learning:** Hands-on engagement > passive listening
- **Narrative Arc:** Tension → Resolution creates memorable moment
- **Concrete > Abstract:** Physical prop > explanation
- **Directed Attention:** "Watch X" guides where brain focuses
- **Failure as Teaching:** Showing what breaks is as valuable as showing what works

## Example Use Case

**Scenario:** You need to explain why autonomous delivery works for high-density areas but not sprawl.

**Confusing concept:** Geographic selectivity (why not everywhere?)

**Prop:** A simple map overlay—transparent layer showing dense zones (dark) and sprawl zones (light)

**Story arc:**
- **Tension:** "Why do other companies fail at autonomous delivery? They try to make it work everywhere."
- **Demonstration:** Place transparent layer on map. "Here's our opportunity: 40% of orders in these dense zones. This is where autonomous works."
- **Resolution:** "We're not trying to solve delivery everywhere. We're solving it *where it matters most*. That's how we win."

**Verbal script:** "I'm going to show you why every autonomous delivery company fails and why we won't. [Place layer on map.] See these dark zones? That's where our high-frequency customers live, short distances, regular orders. That's 40% of our volume and 80% of our margin. The other 60%? Sprawl, one-offs, infrequent. They try to make autonomous work for both. We won't. We'll only deploy where it wins."

## Application in presentation-mastery Skill

This prompt appears in **Phase 3: Content**, used when:
- You have slides explaining complex ideas
- You want to make abstract concepts tangible
- You want a teaching moment that sticks

**Output feeds into:** Either literal props (if presenting live) or high-quality slide illustrations/mockups that approximate the physical prop.

## How to Use This Prompt

1. Identify: What's the ONE confusing aspect of my idea?
2. Run prompt: Input complex concept + audience
3. Design: Physical prop (or verbal equivalent if no prop exists)
4. Script: Exact words to say while demonstrating
5. Practice: Do it 3+ times before presenting (confidence matters)

## Common Pitfalls

- **Too abstract:** "I'll use a metaphor" → Need something physical you can show/point to
- **Over-explaining:** Long story before prop → Lose audience attention before the moment
- **No tension:** Just showing prop → Audience doesn't understand why it matters
- **Prop doesn't work:** Not practicing → Fumbling kills credibility
- **Overkill:** Using props for every slide → Diminishes impact (use sparingly, for key ideas only)
