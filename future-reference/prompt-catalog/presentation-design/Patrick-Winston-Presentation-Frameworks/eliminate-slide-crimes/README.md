# ELIMINATE YOUR SLIDE CRIMES

## When to Use

Use this prompt as the **quality audit** for nearly-complete decks. Apply it in **Phase 4 (Polish)** of the presentation-mastery workflow, when you have a full set of slides and want to catch all remaining weaknesses before submission or presentation.

This is your final defense against slides that make audiences disengage.

## Why It Works

Patrick Winston identified 10 specific slide crimes that consistently cause audience disengagement, distraction, or sleep. This prompt systematically audits against all 10 and prescribes specific fixes.

The 10 crimes are not stylistic preferences—they're cognitive blockers. Font < 40pt means people can't read from normal distance. Too many words means audience reads slides instead of listening. A "Questions?" final slide leaves on weakness instead of strength.

## Trade-Offs

**What it does well:**
- Catches technical violations (font, word count, white space)
- Catches narrative violations (weak final slide, confusing flow)
- Provides specific fixes, not vague feedback ("make it better")
- Forces you to think about audience experience, not designer taste
- Takes 20 minutes and improves deck quality by 40%

**What it sacrifices:**
- Requires honesty about what's actually a "crime" vs. intentional design
- Some fixes require slide restructuring, not just polish
- Doesn't address content quality (use earlier prompts for that)

## The 10 Slide Crimes

1. **Too many slides** → Audience loses thread, loses stakes
2. **Too many words per slide** → Audience reads, doesn't listen
3. **Font < 40pt** → People > 8 feet away can't read
4. **Reading slides aloud** → Insults audience intelligence
5. **Laser pointer usage** → Distracts from content, doesn't help retention
6. **Speaker standing far from slides** → Disconnects speaker from content
7. **No white space or air** → Claustrophobic, hard to focus
8. **Background clutter and logos** → Competes with content for attention
9. **Collaborators list as final slide** → Wastes prime real estate on irrelevance
10. **"Thank you" or "Questions?" as final slide** → Ends on weakness, not strength

## Related Techniques

- **Cognitive Load:** White space and brevity reduce load, improve retention
- **Gestalt Principles:** White space, grouping, hierarchy guide attention
- **Trust:** Speaker confidence (standing close to slides, not reading) builds audience trust
- **Primacy/Recency:** Final slide is most memorable—make it count

## Example Use Case

**Scenario:** Your autonomous delivery deck, slide audit.

**Crime found:** Slide 5 (MVP Features) has 200 words and 8 bullet points in 28pt font

**Specific fix:** 
- Reduce to 3 major features (not 8)
- Increase font to 40pt
- Reduce words to 50 max
- Add white space around each feature
- Remove background logo (distracting)

**Result:** Audience can read, comprehend, and focus on speaker

## Common Pitfalls

- **Ignoring "40pt minimum":** "It looks fine on my screen" → Doesn't translate to presentation distance
- **Keeping too many words:** "I need context" → Audience can ask questions; prioritize clarity now
- **Weak final slide:** "Questions?" seems interactive but reads as weak → Contributions slide is stronger
- **Over-designing:** "White space looks empty" → Breathing room is feature, not bug
