# STRUCTURE ANY TALK THAT PERSUADES

## When to Use

Use this prompt as the **core architecture** for any presentation where you need to convince someone to take action (approve a proposal, fund a project, hire you). Apply it in **Phase 2 (Structure)** of the presentation-mastery workflow, after you've locked your key message but before designing individual slides.

This is the most powerful framework for persuasive presentations because it mirrors how human brains evaluate credibility: "Do I believe their vision?" + "Do I believe they can execute?"

## Why It Works

Patrick Winston's framework maps to how decision-makers actually evaluate proposals:

1. **Vision** (why should I care?) — Establishes problem + approach
2. **Proof of Work** (can they actually do it?) — Demonstrates capability through concrete steps
3. **Contributions** (what do I get?) — Clear call-to-action or decision

Opening + closing mirror each other, creating a psychological loop: audience hears the promise, sees the proof, and arrives at the same conclusion themselves.

## Trade-Offs

**What it does well:**
- Separates "what you want to do" (vision) from "what you've already done" (proof)
- Forces you to lead with vision, not accomplishment (most common mistake)
- Ensures you finish strong (contributions, not "any questions?")
- Makes the argument linear, not scattered
- Works for pitches, case studies, job interviews, product proposals

**What it sacrifices:**
- Requires you to distill your entire argument to 3 components
- Proof section must be specific (vague accomplishments don't work)
- Less room for exploration/tangents (discipline required)

## Related Techniques

- **Narrative Tension:** Vision creates tension, proof resolves it
- **Social Proof:** Proof of work shows you've done this before
- **Call-to-Action:** Contributions slide is explicit, not implicit
- **Mirroring:** Opening and closing reinforce each other

## Example Use Case

**Scenario:** You're pitching autonomous delivery to Instacart leadership.

**Vision:** "The last-mile delivery problem costs us $X annually and alienates customers with wait times. We believe selective autonomous delivery—not replacing all couriers, but targeting high-density, high-frequency orders—can cut fulfillment costs by 30% while improving speed."

**Proof of Work:**
1. Market research shows 3 companies have successfully piloted similar approaches
2. Our analysis identifies 40% of orders fit the autonomous-eligible profile
3. Unit economics show profitability at scale with drone cost of $[X] and delivery margin of $[Y]

**Contributions:** "Approve a 90-day pilot in [city], targeting [metric], with clear success criteria."

## How to Use This Prompt

1. Clarify: What's the specific decision you want from this audience?
2. Run prompt with: goal, audience, desired outcome
3. Draft: Vision statement (2-3 sentences)
4. Draft: 3-5 proof points (specific, not vague)
5. Save: Vision + Proof output, use to structure slide order

## Common Pitfalls

- **Proof without vision:** "Here's what I did" without "Here's why it matters"
- **Vague vision:** "We should think about autonomous delivery" → Audience doesn't know what to do
- **Weak proof:** "I researched this" → Not enough. What did you find? What does it mean?
- **Weak contributions:** "Thank you for listening" → Forgettable. Be specific: "Approve $X budget" or "Implement Y by Z date"
