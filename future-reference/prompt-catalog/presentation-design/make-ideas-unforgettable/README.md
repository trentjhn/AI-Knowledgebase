# MAKE YOUR IDEAS UNFORGETTABLE

## When to Use

Use this prompt to make your **core thesis memorable and repeatable**. Apply it in **Phase 2 (Structure)** of the presentation-mastery workflow, after you've established vision/proof but need your audience to remember one specific idea from your talk.

Use this when you want people to remember your idea weeks later, not just at the end of your presentation.

## Why It Works

Patrick Winston's Star framework weaponizes five dimensions of memorability:

1. **Symbol:** Visual representation (people think in pictures, not words)
2. **Slogan:** Verbal handle (repeatable in conversation without your help)
3. **Surprise:** Counterintuitive insight (breaks existing mental models, makes brain pay attention)
4. **Salient Idea:** The one thing above all others (no competing ideas)
5. **Story:** How it all connects and why it matters (emotional + logical resonance)

Together, these five elements create an idea that's impossible to forget because it activates multiple memory pathways at once.

## Trade-Offs

**What it does well:**
- Makes abstract ideas concrete and visual
- Creates a phrase people repeat ("They said...?")
- Challenges audience assumptions (memorable because surprising)
- Gives you a differentiator in a crowded space
- Works across mediums (slides, verbal, written)

**What it sacrifices:**
- Requires you to be bold and specific (can't hedge)
- Symbol must genuinely represent the idea (no random clever visuals)
- Surprise must be real (fake surprise insults audience intelligence)

## Related Techniques

- **Visual Metaphor:** Symbol > words for retention
- **Cognitive Dissonance:** Surprise > comfort for attention
- **Singularity:** One salient idea > many competing ideas
- **Narrative Transport:** Story > facts for emotional engagement

## Example Use Case

**Scenario:** Your core thesis on autonomous delivery.

**Symbol:** A small circle (representing high-density zones) overlaid on a large city map. Not ALL territory gets autonomous, just the precise zones where it works.

**Slogan:** "Autonomous only where it works"

**Surprise:** "Autonomous delivery succeeds by being selective, not universal. Most companies try to replace all couriers. We'd win by replacing 20% of routes with 80% of the margin."

**Salient Idea:** Geographic selectivity (not universality) is what makes autonomous delivery profitable.

**Story:** "We analyzed every Instacart order in three cities. We found that 40% of orders—high-density, frequent, short-distance—would benefit from autonomous delivery. But 60% wouldn't. The mistake everyone makes is trying to solve for 100% of orders with one solution. We'd target the 40% and get 80% of the benefit with 30% of the cost."

## How to Use This Prompt

1. Clarify: What is the core insight you want people to remember?
2. Run prompt with: core idea, audience, desired memory
3. Draft: Symbol (can be drawn, described, or literal object)
4. Draft: Slogan (< 5 words, repeatable)
5. Identify: What assumption does this challenge?
6. Save: Use Symbol + Slogan on your key slides

## Common Pitfalls

- **Symbol is too abstract:** "Complexity" → Need concrete visual (gears? tangle of string?)
- **Slogan is too long:** "Autonomous delivery is about being selective in geography" → Too wordy. "Selective autonomy" or "Autonomy by zone"
- **Surprise is obvious:** "Weather affects delivery" → Everyone knows. Need something counterintuitive.
- **Multiple salient ideas:** "Autonomous + selective + data-driven" → Pick ONE
- **Story is generic:** "Delivery is hard" → Specific story with numbers
